§ Legal

Know your rights.

Current Florida law as of 2026 — general information, not legal advice. See Legal & Disclosures below for how to reach a real attorney.

01Active Law

Right to record police in public

The First Amendment protects recording officers performing duties in public, subject to reasonable time/place/manner limits. The 11th Circuit (covers Florida) has upheld this repeatedly.

Smith v. City of Cumming (2000) · Crocker v. Beatty (2021)
02Active Law

Florida's "two-party consent" exception

Florida requires all-party consent for private conversations — but on-duty officers conducting public business have no expectation of privacy, so recording them isn't a wiretapping violation.

Fla. Stat. § 934.03 · Waite (Fla. 5th DCA, 2024)
03Updated 2025

The 25-foot "Halo Law"

After a verbal warning, you must not remain within 25 feet of a first responder if intending to interfere. It doesn't ban recording — only requires repositioning if warned.

Fla. Stat. § 843.31 (effective Jan. 1, 2025)
04Active Law

Flyering & distribution zones

Public sidewalks and parks are protected space for advocacy flyers. Private property, including HOA/condo common areas, requires permission — HOAs are not state actors in Florida.

First Amendment public forum doctrine

Quick reference — if you're observing an incident

  1. Stay on public property — sidewalks, streets, and public building exteriors are your strongest ground.
  2. Record video first — it sidesteps the audio-consent question entirely.
  3. Don't physically block or interfere with officer duties — observe from a reasonable distance.
  4. If warned to step back, comply immediately — reposition, keep recording, stay calm.
  5. Log it afterward — date, time, location, unit/badge numbers if visible, footage link.
